The "Minimalist" Recipe LogicInstead of complex mathematical depletion or unit conversions, use a categorical approach.Pantry Staples "Cheat Sheet": Hardcode a list of 20 items (Salt, Pepper, Oil, Flour, Sugar, Garlic, etc.) that the AI always assumes are available. This saves the user from ever having to scan them.The "Rough Quantity" Rule: Don't worry about grams or ounces yet. Use simple counts or percentages (Full, Half, Low).The Prompt Constraint: Instead of asking Gemini to calculate weights, ask it to generate recipes based on names only.The MVP System Prompt"You are a home cook assistant. Given a list of ingredients, suggest 2 simple recipes. You may assume the user has basic oil, salt, and pepper. Constraint: Only suggest recipes where the primary protein and vegetable are already in the list. Format: Title, 5-sentence instructions, and 'Missing' list."
2. The "Semi-Auto" Depletion (The Hack)Calculating exactly how much flour is left after making pancakes is a technical nightmare for an MVP.The MVP Fix: Manual Confirmation on Completion.User clicks "I Made This!"A simple popup appears: "Which of these did you finish? [ ] Eggs [ ] Milk [ ] Spinach"The user taps the ones they used up, and those items are deleted from the DB.Why this works: It’s 100% accurate and requires zero complex math on the backend.
3. Tech Stack SimplificationTo move fast, avoid building a custom GS1 scraper or a complex Vision model from scratch.Vision: Use Gemini 1.5 Flash for everything. It is cheaper and faster than Pro. Don't build a separate barcode logic yet—just have the user take a photo of the item, and let Gemini's multimodal capabilities "read" the label or identify the vegetable in one go.Database: Use a flat list. An item is just a string: "1 carton of eggs" rather than a nested object with weight, unit, and id.
